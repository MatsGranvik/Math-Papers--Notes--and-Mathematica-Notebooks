Counting


.
This is a story I've been meaning to write down for a while.
So - the prelude.
In high school, I independently discovered a handful of important math ideas.
In 9th grade, I was introduced to geometry and proofs , with their focus on terminology and logical deduction. It was the first time that I actively didn't like math.
Sometime during the course of that year, in a chemistry class, some reference to an example or two of probability was made. And, for whatever reason, that idea grabbed me, much more than what we were covering in geometry. By the end of that class period, I had discovered Pascal's Triangle, which I then went on to explore in much greater detail.
I brought my findings to my geometry teacher, who was a very nice person, and she said, "Oh, that's Pascal's Triangle", and that was that. I kind of let the idea drop after finding that out.
I dabbled with my own math investigations over the course of high school, including some very proto binomial transform stuff, but generally my head was more focused on teaching myself programming in C and game programming.
Merging the two, at some point, I spent a few days writing and optimizing C programs to count primes with trial division, the impetus of which I do not recall. I do remember, however, coming up with the idea of wheel factorization while trying to speed up execution. It was nothing extravagent - I'm mostly noting it to say the seeds were already planted.
It was really in 12th grade AP Calculus, though, that set the stage for what I would do later. I liked calculus fine, although the role of analysis and proof still vexed me. Additionally, when dealing with the infinite (as one does in calculus), numerical experiments often are suggestive but not entirely reassuring (which is a great justification for rigorous proofs in the first place, of course).
Sometime around the time we were learning to regurgitate a proof of the Fundamental Theory of Calculus, the text book we were using had an aside about perfect numbers. It was just one of those short side articles math books often have to add a bit of interest, as a break from the main topic they're covering. And that grabbed me. It was exactly the sort of discrete question that was easy to compute (for small values, anyway), but the pattern seemed so utterly elusive otherwise. Was there some methodical way for determining the sum of a whole number's divisors?
For the next two weeks, in that class and in the evening, I worked furiously, trying to figure out a rule that determined the sum of the divisors of a whole number. During that time, my attention to the calculus class evaporated, and my grade started sagging.
But by the end of that two weeks, I had it. I had figured out a way to compute the sum of the divisors of a whole number. If you got the number it in its prime factored form, there was a specific product formula over its prime factors that would compute that sum. And I was thrilled! I had figured it out.
I brought my findings to my calculus teacher, who was a very nice person, and she said, "Oh, I think I recognize that", and a few days later she showed me, in a book she scrounged up, the sum of divisors expressed as the product formula I found. I kind of let the idea drop after finding that out.
This last experience really left a mark on me. I felt wired during those two weeks, my mind just spinning in high gear as I turned the numbers this way and that, trying to puzzle out where those sums came from (and then I got back up to speed in calculus and did fine).
Despite these experiences, I never considered pursuing math vocationally. I participated in Math Counts and some high school AMS contests, and while I did okay, I wasn't especially competitive even locally. No one ever really steered me in those directions too much. And I didn't think about them very much, either; I'd show up, but that exhausted my interest in them.
But really, the truth is, whatever it was that just grabbed me, about Pascal's Triangle, and then grabbed me even harder, about perfect numbers and the divisor function, just wasn't there in the puzzle collections you encounter in those contests. I don't mean to be dismissive in saying that, just that I lack whatever it is that draws people to those kinds of contests and makes them invest in them and get so very competitive. When I got sucked in to the permutations of the Pascal's triangle, or the world of perfect numbers and the divisor function, there was something intensely exploratory about, like I was navigating the edges of some unexplored continent - this was something I never encountered in more formally structured math spaces. That truly profound sense of exploration was the thing.
And besides, in high school, I was busy weighing a future of creative writing or one of programming - and I ended up choosing programming. Math really wasn't on my radar. I don't think I had even an inkling of what people did with math.
