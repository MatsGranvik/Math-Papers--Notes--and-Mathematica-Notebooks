In the course of the previous section's extended explorations, I realized my intuitive counting was keeping me a little muddled. I needed to formalize what I was doing more clearly. And that led me to think a bit more about counting generally.
So I jotted down the following kinds of counting.


This is actually, in retrospect, an extremely interesting pair of papers, in a kind of ironic, dramatic foreshadowing way that would recur again and again. So I'll return to these.
The section labeled "Total Combinations" on the first page describes the Divisor Summatory function. The form on the page highlights my computational orienation at the time; now I'd be much more likely to describe the entry for 3, say, as the count of solutions to $a \cdot b \cdot c \le n$ where $a, b, c \ge 1$ and $a,b,c$ are all whole numbers.
The section labeled "Combinations Excluding 1" on the first page is a counting summatory function closely related to the Divisor Summatory function that lacks a standard name and standard notation. In fact, I've never seen it referenced anywhere. And, again, now I'd be much more likely to describe the entry for 3, say, as the count of solutions to $a \cdot b \cdot c \le n$ where $a, b, c \ge 2$ and $a,b,c$ are all whole numbers.
The section labeled "Unique Combinations" on the second page is a counting function that also lacks a standard name and standard notation. Now I'd be much more likely to describe the entry for 3, say, as the count of solutions to $a \cdot b \cdot c \le n$ where $2 \le a \le b \le c$ and $a,b,c$ are all whole numbers.
My notational habits at this point, I think, hid what were really some straightforward relationships between these three styles of counting.
I spent a little time getting familiar with the Divisor Summatory function at this point. I mapped it out for $D(n)$, and $D_3(n)$,


and for $D_4(n)$ as well.


So I was familiar with it (this will matter hugely later), but I didn't understand how riddled with important properties and symmetries it was, and, coming from my very computationally oriented perspective, I was also aghast at how slow it was to compute in the form I was looking at.
Given the absurd lengths I've gone to since to speed up computing it, this feels ironic in retrospect.
With that said, though, in an important way, the sums I'm labeling "Unique Combinations" also express, essentially, the same core idea that is present when using the Hyperbola method to compute the Divisor Summatory function, which I've also since spent a huge amount of time with, so I think I had some good instincts here.
