A Quick Refresher on Polynomials as Products of Their Zeros

Let's take a very quick break from my visual explanations and introduce just a few math concepts. These will help situate my next few visual explanations.
So, here's a brisk refresher on polynomial zeros, hopefully familiar stuff.
Take this bog standard polynomial, P(x).
$$ P(x) = \frac{1}{24} x^4 - \frac{1}{6} x^3 - \frac{7}{24}x^2 +\frac{11}{12}x + 1$$

$$y=\frac{1}{24} x^4 - \frac{1}{6} x^3 - \frac{7}{24}x^2 +\frac{11}{12}x + 1$$

Now, notice that P(x) equals 0 only when x is -2, -1, 3, and 4 - we know it only has four zeroes from the fundamental theorem of algebra. Well, we can use those zeros to rewrite P(x) in a product form:
$$ P(x) = (1-\frac{x}{-2})(1-\frac{x}{-1})(1-\frac{x}{3})(1-\frac{x}{4})$$
In turn, we can use this to compute any value of x. This suggests knowing the zeros of a polynomial might reveal quite a lot about its general behavior.
Amazingly, given the right conditions, functions with an infinite amount of zeros can also be written as products over their zeros.
As one old and famous example, $\sin( k \cdot \pi ) = 0$ whenever k is an integer. Which is to say, the sine function has an infinite number of zeros.

$$y=\sin(x)$$

And the sine function actually can be expressed as a product of those zeros, as
$$ \sin(x) = x \cdot (1-\frac{x}{\pi})(1-\frac{x}{-\pi})(1-\frac{x}{2 \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{x}{-2 \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{x}{3 \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{x}{-3 \cdot \pi})...$$
This actually works! Here - as an example, watch it in action for sin(6).

$$y=\sin(12) \,\,\,\,\,\,\,\,\textrm{vs}\,\,\,\,\,\,\,\, y=12 \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{\lfloor x \rfloor} (1-\frac{12}{-k \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{12}{k \cdot \pi})$$

I won't explain how this works - resources abound covering this product online - but notice how much this equation resembles, visually, the product form of P(x), above.
