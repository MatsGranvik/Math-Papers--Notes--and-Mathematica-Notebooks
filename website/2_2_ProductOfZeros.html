Let's take a very quick breather from my visual explanations and introduce just a few math concepts. These will help situate my next few visual explanations.
So, here's a brisk refresher on polynomial zeros - hopefully this is familiar stuff.
Take this straightforward polynomial, P(x).
$$ P(x) = \frac{1}{24} x^4 - \frac{1}{6} x^3 - \frac{7}{24}x^2 +\frac{11}{12}x + 1$$
Now, notice, just from looking at the picture, that P(x) equals 0 only when x is -2, -1, 3, and 4 - we know it only has four zeroes from the fundamental theorem of algebra . Well, we can use those zeros to rewrite P(x) in a product form:
$$ P(x) = (1-\frac{x}{-2})(1-\frac{x}{-1})(1-\frac{x}{3})(1-\frac{x}{4})$$
In turn, we can use this formula, this product over the zeros of $P(x)$, to compute any value of $P(x)$... which means knowing the zeros of a polynomial reveals quite a lot about their general behavior.
Amazingly, given the right conditions, functions with an infinite amount of zeros can also be written as products over their zeros.
As one old and famous example, you might remember that $\sin( k \cdot \pi ) = 0$ whenever k is an integer. Which is to say, the sine function has an infinite number of zeros, as the following graph suggests.
And the sine function actually can be expressed as a product of those zeros, as
$$ \sin(x) = x \cdot (1-\frac{x}{\pi})(1-\frac{x}{-\pi})(1-\frac{x}{2 \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{x}{-2 \cdot \pi}) (1-\frac{x}{3 \cdot \pi})(1-\frac{x}{-3 \cdot \pi})...$$
This actually works!
Here - as an example, watch it in action for sin(12).
