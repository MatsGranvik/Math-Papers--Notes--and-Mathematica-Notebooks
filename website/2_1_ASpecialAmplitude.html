2-1: A Very, Very, Very, Very, Very Special Amplitude: $A=-\frac{1}{2}$

Back to 1-3!



So, in section 1, we examined the sum $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{A+f i}$ with different values for $A$ and $f$, and we paid attention to the behavior of a sampling error associated with the sum.
By the end of that section, we had a method of calculating a sampling error for any combination of $A$ and $f$.
But now let's turn our attention to pairs of $A$ and $f$ that produce any sampling error at all.
There's actually two distinct styles of pairs that have this property.
The first style is the case when $A>0$, $f=0$, and $A$ is even, so $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{2}$, $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{4}$, $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{6}$, and so on. We will pass by these without comment. Just accept my word for it that the actual math for showing this is so is, relatively speaking, pretty straight-forward.
But the second set of cases... well, that's a different story entirely. Let's look through five examples of this second style. For all five examples, the value of $A$ happens to be $-\frac{1}{2}$.

$$\sum_{t=1}^x t^{ -\frac{1}{2} + 14.134... i }$$

If you pan down the number line quite a ways, you'll get the impression that $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{ -\frac{1}{2} + 14.134... i }$ doesn't seem to have a sampling error.
Now, a word of warning! This is really pushing the limit of how useful visual intution and sloppy numerical precision can be for us. If I were to show you a graph of the similar case where $A=-.500001$ and $f=14.134...$, that would look very, very much like it didn't have a sampling error either, but in fact its sampling error would be $0.0000913697... + 0.000567815... i$
You have to turn to much more rigorous mathematical techniques to show this, but it is the case that the sampling error for $A=-\frac{1}{2}$ and $f=14.134725141734695...$ is exactly, precisely $0$.
