Some Visual Intuition about the Riemann Zeta Function:
An Introduction


The Riemann Zeta Function is a very famous, very important function in math. A few unanswered questions about it have big prizes attached to them. So let's suppose you're curious about it.
If you are, it's also notoriously tricky to understand without a background in some relatively challenging math. In fact, you can't get far before bumping into cryptic-seeming phrases likes "Analytic Continuation".
Meanwhile, the following related "fact" seems to make the rounds on the internet every few months:
$$1+2+3+4+5+...=-\frac{1}{12}$$
inciting massive bloody flamewars. And that statement seems pretty inscrutable, too.
Okay. Well, masochist that I am, I'm setting myself an incredibly daunting - impossible, really - task for the following few articles. With an absolute MINIMUM of difficult math concepts, I'll try to provide a visual explanation for what the Riemann Zeta Function is, at least from one perspective. My approach will be visually oriented - if you have taken a first year of calculus, you should be good for the actual math, for the most part.
So I won't be rigorous, there's won't be any proofs, and I won't be using any esoteric-but-precise terminology. The "arguments", such as they are, will be visual and intuitive, mostly just interactive graphs and animations. My hope is that by picking some unusual-but-illuminative examples, the Zeta function won't be nearly so mysterious. It'll still be hard to predict, but, if I succeed, you should come away from this series of articles with some good, gut-level sense why it's the most natural thing in the world that it's hard to predict.
And by the end, you'll have a better idea of why that infinite sum of whole numbers gets attached to the number -1/12, too.
And then we'll look at some of the zeros of the function, and I'll even try to show you their relationship to prime numbers. This is fascinating and complicated stuff, without a doubt.
If you want an approach to this general topic that's less a travelogue and relies on more precise terminology and more rigorous mathematical exposition, Terry Tao's exposition here is very good, although the mathematical bar for following it is much higher. This video by 3blue1brown is also great.
So, I want to start with a different sum, one that will be much more useful to explore, visually. What do you suppose the following sum equals?
$$1^{ 100 i }+2^{ 100 i }+3^{ 100 i }+4^{ 100 i }+5^{ 100 i }+...=?$$
Your intuition about complex exponentials might not be great. Your intuition about trigonometry functions might be a little better, but lucky for both of us, function like sine and cosine also lend themselves very nicely to some illuminating visuals. So let's convert this sum into some nice, easily visualized trigonometry. Using summation notation and the familiar rules $x^t=e^{t \log x}$ and Euler's formula $e^{i x} = \cos x + i \sin x$, we can convert this into
$$1^{ 100 i }+2^{ 100 i }+3^{ 100 i }+4^{ 100 i }+...=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{ 100 i }= \sum_{t=1}^\infty e^{ 100 (\log t) \cdot i }=\sum_{t=1}^\infty \cos( 100 \log t ) + i \sin( 100 \log t)$$
And now I make the preposterous assertion that there might be some way to make sense of the claim that these last two sums on the right equal
$$\sum_{t=1}^\infty \cos( 100 \log t ) + i \sin( 100 \log t)=6.51721...-.181288... \cdot i$$
Which is to say,
$$\cos(100 \log 1 )+\cos(100 \log 2 )+\cos(100 \log 3 )+\cos(100 \log 4 )+\cos(100 \log 5 )+...=6.51721...$$ $$i \cdot(\sin(100 \log 1 )+\sin(100 \log 2 )+\sin(100 \log 3 )+\sin(100 \log 4 )+\sin(100 \log 5 )+...)=-.181288... \cdot i$$
Although $1+2+3+4+5+...=-{1 \over 12}$ is more obviously absurd, these sums are pretty silly too. The cosine of real arguments is always been -1 and 1, so the terms being added to this sum keep oscillating between 1 and -1 without ever getting any smaller. This can't possibly converge, you might very well argue.
And you're right! It doesn't converge. So saying that the sum $=6.51721...$ must be nonsense, right?
Well, yes. And in the most important, obvious sense, that's the answer, and it's important to recognize that.
But there's also a way in which the number 6.51721... is deeply connected to this series. That connection is actually really, really easy to see if partial sums of the sum are graphed, so let's do that. The red plot will be the real sum, and the blue plot will be the imaginary sum.
This graph is interactive, so play around with it, especially as $x$ gets bigger. Click and drag to move around, and use the mouse wheel to zoom in and out, or, if you don't have a mousewheel, double tap to zoom in, and hold shift and double tap to zoom out.
(And convince yourself that both the real and imaginary sums behave in similar ways - going forward, I'll only focus on the real parts of these sums for simplicity. But everything that follows applies to the imaginary sum as well.)
So... what do you see? Well, the early part of the graph looks like like a random walk, right? Say, until $x=20$ or so. Then, as $x$ gets larger and larger, the $y$ eventually looks like a quantized wave, the amplitude of which gets bigger and bigger. It does not, in fact, converge to a particular value. But, there does seem to be a value the wave centered on. And what is that value? Visually, it's in the ballpark of six and a half. If I claimed it was $6.51721...$, you wouldn't put up too much resistance, I bet.
Really drag the graph out to the right a ways to convince yourself that the wave, despite growing in amplitude, stays centered on that value. So while the wave doesn't converge, its center seems to.
Imagine, for a moment, we had a special number type, a wave number, which had an oscillating part and a center-of-the-wave part (much as complex numbers have a real part and an imaginary part). Given such a number system, you might concede, on the basis of the above graph, that the center part of $\cos(100 \log 1 )+\cos(100 \log 2 ) +\cos(100 \log 3 )+\cos(100 \log 4 )+\cos(100 \log 5 )+...$ does seem to converge to something like $6.51721...$.
But let's stop for a second. Why, exactly, does the wave end up centered on $6.51721...$ anyway? Is there a way to think more clearly about that? And why does that happen around $x=20$? And can we describe more explicitly the oscillating part?
Here's a pretty straightforward and fruitful experiment.
Let's compare our sum, $\sum_{t=1}^x t^{ 100 i }$, with the integral $\int_{0}^x t^{ 100 i } dt$, and see how they're related.
One of the first rules you encounter in calculus is the power rule for derivatives: $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}x^z = z \cdot x^{z-1}$. Its antiderivative is, in turn, just the inverse of this: $\int x^z dx = \frac{x^{z+1}}{z+1}+C$. (This works just fine for real numbers raised to complex powers). This lets us compute the definite integral $\int_{0}^x t^{ 100 i } dt$ as $\frac{x^{1+100 i}}{1+100 i} - \frac{0^{1+100 i}}{1+100 i}$. That second term is just 0, so $$\int_{0}^x t^{ 100 i } dt = \frac{x^{1+100 i}}{1+100 i}$$
For the sake of graphing and visual intuition, we can split this into real and imaginary parts as
$$\int_{0}^x t^{ 100 i } dt = \int_0^x \cos( 100 \log t ) + i \cdot \int_0^x \sin( 100 \log t )$$
which in turn are
$$\int_0^x \cos( 100 \log t ) = \frac{x}{10001} \cdot (\cos(100 \log x) + 100 \sin( 100 \log x ))$$
and
$$i \cdot \int_0^x \sin( 100 \log t ) = i \frac{x}{10001} \cdot (\sin(100 \log x) - 100 \cos( 100 \log x ))$$
So let's compare the real parts of our sum and our integral.
Look further down the x-axis, around $x=500$. The oscillating part of the sum quite obviously has the same frequency and amplitude as the wave described by the integral. It just has an offset center. The integral is centered on 0 - look closely at $\frac{x}{10001} \cdot (\cos(100 \log x) + 100 \sin( 100 \log x ))$ to see why that's the case.
So what does this have to do with the wave ending up centered on $6.51721...$?
Well, to think more clearly about that question, let's look more explicitly at what is being added, specifically, as $x$ increases.
The obvious observation is that the red sum is taking whole number sized samples of the smooth, continuous blue curve.
Because the blue curve is the cosine of the logarithm of $x$ (scaled by a factor), its frequency (forgive me for abusing this term a bit in the following articles) is very, very high for small $x$ and then grows slower as $x$ gets larger. Think about why that's true.
Okay. Now, let's look at three regions of this graph.
First, look around $x=500$. The red sum does a fine job approximating the shape of the smooth blue curve.
Now look at the range $x=0$ to $5$. Here, the blue cosine wave goes up and down very, very quickly, with many, many peaks coming and going in an individual red sample. The red sum and smooth blue curve have no obvious relation, the red sum is sampling the blue curve so randomly.
Finally, and most intriguingly, look at the range $x=13$ to $20$. Here, the red sum is also doing a terrible job approximating the smooth blue curve underneath it. Nevertheless, the red sum is no longer behaving in a random fashion. In fact, all of the samples seem biased in one direction, as though it were sampling a different, much lower frequency curve.
And in fact, notice the line labeled 1hz. That's the $x$ value where the smooth blue curve is making exactly 1 entire cycle per single red sample. This is why the sampling error all accumulates in one direction. You can see a similar, though smaller, bias at the line marked 2hz.
Okay. So what do these three regions have to do with the sum ending up centered on $=6.51721...$?
Well, in areas around $x = 500$ and beyond, the red sum approximates the smooth blue curve pretty well. A good hunch might be that the frequency at which the red sum samples the blue curve exceeds the frequency of the blue curve itself in some important way. Anyway, the center of the red sum already seems established by this point. So this probably tells us something about why the center converges, but not why it drifts originally.
On the other end of the graph, until $x= 6$, the red samples seem almost random. This probably contributes to the drift on the center, but in hard-to-discern-ways.
And between those two regions, centered on $x = 15$ and, to a lesser extent, $x = 8$, the red sum acts as though it is sampling some other, much lower frequency curve than the blue curve it's actually sampling, causing the center of the red sum to take a long shift in one vertical direction.
If you've ever worked with animation, image processing, or audio processing - anything involving sampling, either temporally or spatially, really - this might look naggingly familiar. In fact, something like temporal aliasing is broadly responsible for the center drifting generally, something like the Nyquist frequency relates to why the center stops drifting once the change in frequency of the continuous curve slows down enough, and the wagon-wheel effect is related to why the areas at x=15 and x=8 seem as though they are being sampled from some other continuous curve that is changing at a lower frequency (or see here too).
So, to restate where we've gone here...
Given the sum we started with
$$\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{100 i} \neq 6.51721...-.181288... \cdot i$$
because that sum doesn't converge, as all the above graphs have strongly suggested.
However, quite visibly, if we compare summing $t^{100 i}$ over the reals versus summing it over the whole numbers, there IS something very much like a sampling error that accumulates that recenters the eventual non-converging wave in the discrete case, and that continuous-to-discrete sampling error in this case is $6.517210426253009... - 0.18128842533808529...\cdot i$ .
Given what we've just covered, it is tempting to think we might isolate the sampling error by subtracting our continuous wave $\int_0^x \cos( 100 \log t ) \,dt$ from our discrete wave $\sum_{t=1}^{\lfloor x \rfloor} \cos( 100 \log t )$. So let's see what happens if we do that.
And this is indeed a useful idea. Although this doesn't quite converge (but note that it does remain bounded!), this graph does make the role of $=6.51721...$ here especially clear. We will return to the idea of subtracting the continuous wave from the discrete one, which is very important, later.
Okay. Now I'm going to change subjects quite drastically, without warning. I'm going to open up Wolfram Alpha, and I'll ask it to calculate the Riemann Zeta function for $-100 i$ by typing zeta(-100 i) (the minus sign is because the Zeta function expects function arguments in a very slightly different form, but this is trivial and not worth commenting on). And it says... let's see...
$$\zeta(-100 i) = 6.517210426253009... - 0.18128842533808529... i$$
Well would you look at that.
For the sake of intuition about this sampling error, let's connect it to another very familiar, much simpler concept.
Earlier, I said you could think of $\sum_{t=1}^{\lfloor x \rfloor} \cos( 100 \log t )$ as being represented by a special two-part number, with a non-converging wave part and a converging center part / sampling error.
The continuous equivalent, $\int_0^x \cos( 100 \log t ) \,dt$, on the other hand, is centered on 0. It doesn't have a sampling error (in fact, this is tautological, given the definition of Riemann integrals).
Actually, it's not much work at all to see that the waves generated by the generalized continuous case , $\int_0^x t^A \cdot \cos( f \log t ) \,dt$, are all centered on 0 for any $A$ and $f$. So, if we only ever worked with the continuous case, we would never even think to trot out special two-part numbers, because it never, ever has a center other than 0. Only the discrete case requires special two-part numbers.
This is not actually the first time you've encountered this pattern.
In fact, when you were first taught whole number division in grade school, you were told the answer is a special two-part number, with a whole number quotient and a whole number remainder. And that probably made division seem complicated and mysterious, but you got used to it.
And then, later, you were taught about fractions and real numbers. And in an instant, remainders disappeared, and division just resulted in a nice, tidy quotient. The need for two-part numbers to express answers vanished once you were working with nice, continuous real numbers.
What's happening here, with our sum $\sum_{t=1}^{\lfloor x \rfloor} \cos( 100 \log t )$, with its wave part and its sampling error part, is rather similar in spirit, to the quotient and remainder of whole number division. Don't try to push this analogy too far, but I think this comparison might get you in the right frame of mind.
(And in fact, the remainders of whole number division can themselves usefully be thought of as a sampling error too - we'll return to this idea in a later article)
Okay. So next we'll watch what happens to the sampling error as we replace our term $100 i$ with other values.
