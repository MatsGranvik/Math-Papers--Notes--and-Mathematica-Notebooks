Alright. Let's speed things up a bit. In the first article, we looked at the sum $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{100 i}$ and saw that, while the sum didn't have a limit as x approaches infinity, it did have a what I'm calling a sampling error that converged, and that sampling error equaled the value of the Riemann Zeta function at $-100 i$. In the second article, we investigated the sampling errors when the value of $100 i$ increased or decreased to other imaginary values.
So we explored raising our samples to imaginary powers. So let's turn to complex powers. We'll investigate scaling the amplitude of the samples by real-valued powers as well. So, instead of merely looking at $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{1000 i}$, we'll investigate $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{1+1000 i}$, and $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{-\frac{1}{2}+100 i}$, and $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{-2+100 i}$, and so on. More generally, this will be $\sum_{t = 1}^x t^{A+f i}$, with $A$ our amplitude and $f$ our frequency.
Connecting back to trigonometry, another form for this sum is
$$1^{ A+f i }+2^{ A+f i }+3^{ A+f i }+4^{ A+f i }+...=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{ A+f i }=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{ A } \cdot t^{ f i }=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^A \cdot e^{ f (\log t) \cdot i }=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^A \cdot \cos( f \log t ) + i \cdot \sum_{t=1}^\infty t^A \cdot \sin( f \log t)$$
Just to be painfully explicit, that means that
$$1^{ 1+1000 i }+2^{ 1+1000 i }+3^{ 1+1000 i }+4^{ 1+1000 i }+...=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t \cdot \cos( 1000 \log t ) + i \cdot \sum_{t=1}^\infty t \cdot \sin( 1000 \log t)$$
$$1^{ \frac{1}{4}+1000 i }+2^{ \frac{1}{4}+1000 i }+3^{ \frac{1}{4}+1000 i }+4^{ \frac{1}{4}+1000 i }+...=\sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{\frac{1}{4}} \cdot \cos( 1000 \log t ) + i \cdot \sum_{t=1}^\infty t^{\frac{1}{4}} \cdot \sin( 1000 \log t)$$
$$1^{ -\frac{1}{2}+1000 i }+2^{ -\frac{1}{2}+1000 i }+3^{ -\frac{1}{2}+1000 i }+4^{ -\frac{1}{2}+1000 i }+...=\sum_{t=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}} \cdot \cos( 1000 \log t ) + i \cdot \sum_{t=1}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{t}} \cdot \sin( 1000 \log t)$$
and so on.
